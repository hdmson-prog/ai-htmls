Carbon neutrality achieved across all estate operations

After eight years of systematic improvements, Heritage Tea Manufactory has achieved carbon neutrality across cultivation, processing, and distribution. This milestone reflects our commitment to stewardship and long-term thinking.

December 15, 2024 5 min read By Estate Management

Solar installation and biodiversity corridors across the estate

In 2016, we committed to achieving carbon neutrality within a decade. This week, we are pleased to confirm that goal has been reached—two years ahead of schedule.

The path to carbon neutrality was not a single dramatic gesture, but rather a series of deliberate, measured improvements across every aspect of our operations. We approached the challenge as we approach tea processing: with patience, precision, and a willingness to invest in what endures.

The baseline

We began with a comprehensive carbon audit conducted by an independent third party. The results were sobering. Our estate operations produced approximately 2,400 metric tons of CO₂ equivalent annually, primarily from:

  • Fossil fuel consumption in tea processing (firing, drying, packaging)
  • Diesel generators for backup power
  • Transportation and distribution logistics
  • Fertilizer application (even organic methods release some emissions)
  • Water heating and facility operations

Rather than purchase carbon offsets, we decided to pursue actual reductions. Offsets have their place, but we believed our responsibility was to change our methods, not simply pay for the privilege of continuing them.

"True sustainability is not about offsetting impact—it is about fundamentally rethinking how we operate. That requires time, investment, and a willingness to challenge comfortable assumptions."

— Aravinda Perera, Managing Director

Energy transformation

The most significant intervention was energy. Over three years, we installed 480 solar panels across processing facility rooftops and unused land parcels. These panels now generate 85% of our electricity needs. On days when production is low, we contribute excess power to the regional grid.

Backup power—essential for continuous processing—was converted from diesel to battery storage systems charged by solar during the day. This eliminated 340 tons of annual emissions.

For thermal energy (required for tea firing and drying), we transitioned from fossil fuels to biomass. Tea production generates substantial organic waste: stems, dust, and low-grade leaf unsuitable for final products. We now combust this material in controlled systems, converting waste into heat energy. The process is carbon-neutral, as the released carbon was absorbed during the plant's growth.

Water and soil

We implemented a rainwater harvesting system that captures and filters monsoon rainfall. This reduced groundwater pumping (which requires energy) by 60%. The system stores 2 million liters, enough to supply processing needs during dry months.

Soil management shifted toward regenerative practices. Composting estate waste creates fertilizer without purchasing chemical inputs (which have high embedded carbon costs). Cover cropping between tea rows reduces erosion and increases soil carbon sequestration. Over time, healthy soil becomes a carbon sink rather than a source.

Rainwater collection infrastructure across the estate

Transportation

Distribution is complex. We export to 47 countries, and transportation is inherently carbon-intensive. However, we made improvements:

  • Consolidated shipping schedules to maximize container efficiency
  • Partnered with carbon-neutral freight carriers where available
  • Reduced packaging weight by 15% through material optimization
  • Transitioned estate vehicles to hybrid and electric models

For remaining unavoidable emissions (primarily international freight), we invested in certified reforestation projects in Sri Lanka. This is not offset for offset's sake—it is restoration of degraded land adjacent to our estate, creating biodiversity corridors and watershed protection.

Verification

In November 2024, an independent auditor verified our calculations using ISO 14064 standards. The audit confirmed net-zero emissions across Scope 1 (direct operations) and Scope 2 (purchased energy). Scope 3 (indirect supply chain emissions) remains an area of ongoing work, though we have reduced it by 40% since 2016.

Certification matters. We are not interested in unverifiable claims or creative accounting. The numbers are public, the methodology is transparent, and the audit reports are available upon request.

What this means

Carbon neutrality is not an endpoint. It is a milestone within a longer commitment to operating responsibly. The work continues: improving efficiency, reducing waste, supporting ecosystem health, and ensuring our operations benefit rather than burden the land we depend on.

Our partners and customers have asked whether this will affect pricing. The honest answer: marginally. The investments were substantial, but they were made incrementally over eight years. Solar panels pay for themselves through energy savings. Biomass systems eliminate fuel costs. Water harvesting reduces pumping expenses. In some cases, sustainability and financial prudence align.

We share this not as marketing, but as documentation. Other estates and manufacturers may find value in our approach. We are happy to discuss methods, challenges, and results with industry peers. This is not competitive advantage—it is collective responsibility.

"Our children and grandchildren will inherit this estate. We have an obligation to leave it better than we found it—not as rhetoric, but as measurable fact."

— Nisha Jayawardena, Sustainability Officer

Looking forward

The next phase focuses on supply chain partners. We are working with packaging suppliers to reduce embedded carbon in materials. Shipping partners are transitioning to lower-emission vessels. Even our office operations are under review—electricity, paper, waste, everything.

By 2030, we aim to achieve carbon negativity, meaning the estate sequesters more carbon than it emits. This requires expanding forest corridors, deepening soil carbon programs, and continuing to optimize every aspect of operations.

It is ambitious. But eight years ago, carbon neutrality seemed ambitious too. Patience, precision, and persistence tend to yield results.
